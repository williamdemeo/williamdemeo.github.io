It Walks Like a Duck...

by William DeMeo - Feb 13th, 2014 - posted in Articles

It’s easy to construct two finite algebras that are the same except for a relabelling of the operations. Our intuition tells us that these are simply different names for the same algebraic structure. But according to the classical definitions, not only are such algebras distinct mathematical objects, but also they are nonisomorphic!

Isotopy is another well known notion of “sameness” that is broader than isomorphism. However, I recently proved a result establishing that isotopy is not a notion of algebraic equivalence.

$\def\bA{\bf A} \def\bB{\bf B}$

Here is an example of two algebraic structures that are obviously the same except for a relabelling of the operations: let $\bA$ be an algebra of size 4 and label the elements of the universe ${1,2,3,4}$. Suppose $\bA$ has two unary operations, which we label $f^\bA$ and $g^\bA$, and suppose $f^\bA$ is the permutation $(1,2)(3,4)$ and $g^\bA$ is the permutation $(1,3)(2,4)$.

Now let $\mathbf{B}$ be defined in almost the same way except we let $f^\bB$ be the permutation $(1,3)(2,4)$ and let $g^\bA$ be the permutation $(1,2)(3,4)$.

Clearly we have simply swapped the names of the operations, and any sane person would agree that we have two “equivalent” algebraic structures. But these are not considered equivalent, and they are not even isomorphic according to the classical definition.

The Isotopy GitHub repository contains an article in which I present algebras that are isotopic and yet have vastly different congruence lattices.

Congruence relations of algebras are the kernels of homomorphisms, and the congruence lattice is a basic “algebraic” feature of an algebra. Certainly we can’t accept a notion of “sameness” that equates two algebras with different congruence lattices.
